Moving Objects — Introduction

Objects are said to be in motion when their position changes with time compared to another object used as reference. Motion is everywhere: walking, cars on a road, a falling leaf. We will learn how to describe motion and measure it.

Key concepts

  • Reference point — we need something fixed to say whether an object moved.
  • Distance vs displacement — distance is path length; displacement is straight-line change in position.
  • Types of motion — linear, circular, oscillatory.

Why it matters

  • Describing motion helps us design safe roads, sports strategies and machines.
  • Understanding measurement and speed is useful in everyday life and science.

Measurement of length & distance

We measure how far objects are or how long they are using units and tools. Learn SI units, tools like ruler, measuring tape, and how to measure carefully.

Basic rules

  • Standard unit in SI: metre (m). Subunits: centimetre (cm), millimetre (mm).
  • Use the correct tool: ruler for small lengths, tape for larger distances.
  • Read the measuring scale from the zero mark; keep the instrument straight for accurate reading.

Practical tips & activities

  • Activity: measure the length of a book in cm using a ruler — record three readings and take the average.
  • Estimate first, then measure — improves skill in judging distances.

Speed

Speed tells how fast an object moves. We'll learn the formula, units and simple calculations to find speed from distance and time.

Basic formula

  • Speed = Distance ÷ Time.
  • SI unit: metres per second (m/s). Common unit: kilometres per hour (km/h).
  • Convert: 1 m/s = 3.6 km/h (multiply by 3.6).

Examples & practice

  • If a runner covers 100 m in 20 s, speed = 100 ÷ 20 = 5 m/s.
  • Activity: time a toy car over 2 m and calculate speed in m/s.
